What is International Mother Language Day?

UNESCO Initiative

Proclaimed by UNESCO in 1999 and observed annually on February 21, this day aims to promote the preservation and protection of all languages used by peoples of the world.

Cultural Significance

It recognizes that languages are vital to cultural identity, education, social integration, and sustainable development.

What is mother language?

A mother language, or first language, is the initial language a person learns from birth, typically spoken at home and taught by family. It is deeply tied to one’s identity, culture, and emotions, serving as the primary tool for communication and self-expression. This language carries the traditions, values, and history of a community, making it a vital part of personal and cultural heritage. Preserving mother languages is crucial for maintaining global linguistic diversity and ensuring future generations stay connected to their roots. Losing a mother language can mean losing a piece of cultural identity. Celebrating and protecting these languages helps sustain the richness of human expression and knowledge, fostering understanding and respect across different cultures. Mother languages are the foundation of who we are.

If you talk to a man in a language he understands, that goes to his head. If you talk to him in his language, that goes to his heart.

― Nelson Mandela

Language preservation

Language preservation is the vital effort to protect and sustain endangered languages from disappearing forever. With one language dying every two weeks, the world is losing not just words but entire systems of knowledge, culture, and identity that have been passed down for generations. Each language holds unique ways of understanding the world, from traditional practices to ecological knowledge, and its loss is irreversible. To preserve languages, we can take active steps such as documenting them through audio and video recordings, creating written resources like dictionaries and grammar books, and supporting community-led initiatives that encourage their use in homes, schools, and public spaces. Teaching these languages to younger generations is especially important, as they are the key to keeping them alive. Governments, organizations, and individuals can also promote multilingual education and create policies that support linguistic diversity. By valuing and revitalizing endangered languages, we not only honor the cultures they represent but also enrich the global tapestry of human expression and understanding.
